Qualitative Research

A visual exploration of its core purposes and diverse applications, bringing clarity to how we understand complex human experiences and social phenomena.

Purposes of Qualitative Research

Generating New Theory

Develops new theories or conceptual frameworks to explain unexplored or poorly understood phenomena through in-depth investigation.

Developing or Challenging Theory

Refines or expands existing theories by providing new perspectives, revealing complexities, or highlighting inaccuracies.

Proposing Practical Implications

Yields actionable insights to inform policy, practice, and intervention strategies based on real-world experiences.

Enhancing Understanding & Empathy

Promotes deeper understanding of human experiences, emotions, and perspectives, fostering empathy and cultural sensitivity.

Informing Mixed-Methods Research

Complements quantitative research by generating hypotheses, informing survey design, or providing context for numerical results.

Examples of Qualitative Research

Dynamics of Online Communities

Exploring culture, social dynamics, interaction patterns, communication styles, and shared values within virtual communities (often ethnographic/netnographic).

Example Methodological Steps:

  • Select a specific online community for study.
  • Gain entry and immerse in the community (observing, sometimes participating).
  • Collect data: digital field notes, screenshots of interactions, archival posts.
  • Analyze data for cultural norms, language use, social structures, and rituals.
  • Develop an "insider" understanding of the community's dynamics.

Women in STEM Careers

Investigating career experiences, pathways, success factors, barriers, and challenges for women in STEM fields (often narrative research).

Example Methodological Steps:

  • Recruit women from diverse STEM fields and career stages.
  • Conduct in-depth narrative interviews, prompting them to share their career stories.
  • Transcribe interviews; potentially "restory" narratives with participants.
  • Analyze narratives for common themes, turning points, and influential factors.
  • Present findings as individual or composite stories that illuminate their experiences.

Social Media in Political Activism

Exploring social media's role in shaping political activism, public opinion, communication, mobilization, and discourse (document/visual analysis).

Example Methodological Steps:

  • Define the scope (e.g., specific movement, platform, time period).
  • Collect relevant social media data (posts, images, videos) using ethical methods.
  • Apply qualitative content analysis, visual analysis, or discourse analysis to the data.
  • Identify themes, frames, narratives, and communication strategies.
  • Interpret how social media is used in the context of political activism.

AI-Assisted Video Content Analysis

Utilizing Machine Learning (ML) to process and analyze large video datasets for qualitative insights, where techniques like Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) within ML models help understand sequential video data.

Example Methodological Steps:

  • Define research questions suitable for large-scale video analysis (e.g., patterns in communication, representation of groups, recurring behaviors).
  • Collect and curate a relevant video dataset, ensuring ethical considerations for data privacy and use.
  • Preprocess video data: segment videos, extract frames, transcribe audio.
  • Employ ML models (e.g., Transformers with RoPE for sequence understanding) for tasks like:
    • Automated transcription and speaker diarization.
    • Object, action, and scene recognition.
    • Sentiment analysis or topic modeling on transcribed speech or visual cues.
    • Identifying patterns or anomalies across the dataset.
  • Qualitatively analyze and interpret ML outputs: review auto-generated summaries, identified clips, or statistical patterns. The ML findings become data points for deeper human-led qualitative inquiry.
  • Triangulate ML findings with other qualitative data or researcher observations.
  • Report findings, acknowledging the role of AI tools and their limitations.
